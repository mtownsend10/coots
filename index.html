The platform

Three priorities. In this order.

A County Council seat is not a social-media account. It is a line on a vote and a position in a room. Here is what I will vote for, push for, and build coalitions around — in plain language, with the mechanisms that actually move the needle.

  1. 01

    Housing. First, and last.

    The median Kauaʻi home has crossed a million dollars. Nurses, teachers, firefighters, farmers, and the kids who grew up here are being priced off their own island. If the Council does not put housing at the center of every decision for the next ten years, we will lose the Kauaʻi we grew up with. I will put it there.

    What I’ll do on day one.

    • Implement Act 39 (SB 3202) with local guardrails. State law requires counties to allow two ADUs per residential lot by December 31, 2026. Our ordinance must come with owner-occupancy and long-term-rental conditions so local families build, not out-of-state LLCs.
    • Scale Lima Ola on a clear timeline. The county’s flagship workforce-housing project has delivered homes to 23 families. The next 500+ units need a published build-and-deliver schedule, not another master-plan refresh.
    • Three-tier residential-investor property tax. Progressive rates for homes under $1M, $1M–$3M, and over $3M — modeled on successful Hawaiʻi-county approaches. Reward owner-occupancy. Discourage corporate hoarding.
    • Vacant-home tax on second homes held empty more than 180 days a year. Revenue goes directly to the Housing Trust Fund.
    • Phase down non-VDA vacation rentals. Use the authority the state gave counties in Act 18 (2024) to return grandfathered inventory outside Visitor Destination Areas to long-term housing on a defined, fair amortization schedule.
    • Remove minimum lot sizes for multifamily in town cores. Līhuʻe, Kapaʻa, Kōloa. Gentle density where infrastructure already exists — not another subdivision on ag land.
    • Deed-restricted, owner-occupied ownership programs. County-supported pathways to homeownership that stay affordable through resale, not just to the first buyer.
  2. 02

    Wastewater & infrastructure that actually work.

    Our wastewater system is aging, our cesspool-conversion obligations under Act 125 are enormous, Kūhiō Highway is one landslide from cutting the North Shore off, and our bus runs too few hours for service-industry workers. Infrastructure is unglamorous. It is also the thing that either lets families live here or doesn’t.

    • Cesspool-to-septic/sewer conversion. Kauaʻi is behind on Act 125’s 2050 deadline. Stand up a county cost-share and low-interest loan program so low-income homeowners can comply without losing their homes.
    • Fund Wailua & East Kauaʻi wastewater upgrades — aging plants, aging lines, and a coastline that can’t afford another failure event.
    • Kūhiō corridor multimodal redesign. Protected bike lanes, bus priority, and the completion of the next phases of Ke Ala Hele Makalae. Widening alone is not a plan.
    • Kauaʻi Bus expansion. Evening and weekend service for service-industry workers who can’t currently get to a 9pm shift on the bus.
    • Proactive PFAS testing at every county well — before it becomes a crisis.
  3. 03

    Homelessness and quality of life.

    The share of our unhoused neighbors living with chronic mental illness and substance-use disorder is rising. Fentanyl is arriving alongside the methamphetamine that has hurt this island for years. We are not going to arrest our way out. We are going to house, treat, and walk with people out.

    • Housing-first, with wraparound services. Fund a real pipeline of permanent supportive housing — not just shelter beds.
    • Mobile crisis response. Mental-health calls answered by clinicians and peer specialists, not police. It is better for the person in crisis and better for KPD.
    • Expand dual-diagnosis capacity. Partner with the state to fund Kauaʻi Community Mental Health Center and local nonprofits doing the work now on threadbare budgets.
    • Harm reduction. Naloxone access and fentanyl test strips distributed through community partners. These save lives on Wednesday night.
    • Parks, beaches, and public spaces that residents can actually use — including priority-access systems for residents at the busiest sites, expanded after the Hāʻena model.

These are the priorities that come first. I have longer views on the ocean, on cultural protection, on agriculture, and on tourism — I will keep writing about them as the campaign goes on. But if I am on the Council, and only three things get my full weight behind them, it is these three.

About Mike

A Kīlauea small-business owner,
and someone who has moved hard laws.

Mike Coots was born and raised on Kauaʻi and lives in Kīlauea. He owns and operates a small photography business that works with local hotels, small businesses, and the visitor bureau. He is a neighbor before he is anything else.

In 1997, at eighteen, a tiger shark attacked him bodyboarding off the westside and he lost his right leg. Most people know that part. What came after is the part that prepared him for this work.

A decade later, he picked up a camera — and then a cause. He helped pass Hawaiʻi’s first-in-the-nation ban on the shark-fin trade in 2010. He lobbied Congress for the federal Shark Conservation Act, which President Obama signed into law in 2011. He has testified at the United Nations and before committees in Washington. His 2023 book Shark: Portraits (Rizzoli) is eight years of that work.

The point is not the sharks. The point is the pattern: show up prepared, build a coalition across political lines, write the bill in plain language, and keep showing up until the vote happens. That is the skill set this Council needs on housing. It is what Mike is bringing to the job.

— Mike

Why 2026

Four of seven seats are open. This election decides the next decade.

For the first time since 2002, Kauaʻi County Council has four open seats in a single cycle. The mayor’s office is changing hands. The laws we pass on housing, wastewater, and homelessness will shape this island long past our time on the Council.

I’m running because I’ve spent a career taking on hard fights and winning them — not for an ideology, but for a place. Now the place is home.
