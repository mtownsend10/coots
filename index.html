My story

From the reef off Majors Bay
to the people’s work.

I grew up in Kalāheo, raised by an island that teaches you early: we take care of the ocean, and the ocean takes care of us. In October 1997, at eighteen, a tiger shark attacked me bodyboarding off the westside. I lost my right leg. I was in the water again five weeks later.

What came after the attack is the part I didn’t expect — a decade later, I picked up a camera, then a cause. I’ve spent my career photographing sharks the way we photograph people: up close, honestly, with respect. I’ve testified before the U.S. Congress, lobbied at the United Nations, and helped pass the nation’s first state ban on the shark-fin trade right here in Hawaiʻi. In 2011, I stood alongside survivors from around the world as President Obama signed the federal Shark Conservation Act into law.

My book Shark: Portraits (Rizzoli, 2023) is eight years of that work. But the most important photograph I’ll ever make is the Kauaʻi my kids inherit — an island where working families can afford to live, where reefs are alive, where visitors are welcomed and residents come first.

That’s why I’m running. Not to be a politician. To be a neighbor at the table.

— Mike

The platform

Seven priorities for a working Kauaʻi.

Practical, progressive, rooted in the place. Here’s what I’ll work on, in plain language.

  1. 01

    Housing our workforce — first.

    Nurses, teachers, firefighters, farmers, and kids who grew up here can’t afford to live here. I’ll push to scale what works (Lima Ola, ARU) and fix what doesn’t: close the ADU loophole that lets investors absorb units meant for local families, tax long-vacant second homes, and use the state’s new authority to convert idle vacation-rental stock back to housing.

    • Require ADUs built with county incentives to be long-term rentals for residents.
    • Expand deed-restricted, owner-occupied workforce ownership.
    • Comply with Act 39 on ADUs — with guardrails so locals, not LLCs, benefit.
  2. 02

    A tourism economy that works for residents.

    Tourism is part of our economy. It shouldn’t run our lives. The Hāʻena reservation system proved we can protect a place, respect residents, and still welcome visitors. I’ll work to bring that model to the beaches getting crushed next: Tunnels, Polihale, Queen’s Bath, Kalapaki.

    • Priority, free access for residents at managed sites.
    • Use SB 2919 (Act 18) authority to phase down non-VDA vacation rentals on a clear, fair timeline.
    • Raise TVR tax classification and enforce owner-occupancy rules.
  3. 03

    Protect the ocean. It protects us.

    Reefs, coasts, and the life they hold are Kauaʻi’s most important infrastructure. 72% of our sandy shorelines are retreating. I’ll work for managed retreat, stronger coastal setbacks, a Maui-style ban on chemical sunscreens, and real enforcement of the marine protections we already have.

    • No new construction permits in projected V-zones.
    • Maui-equivalent mineral-only sunscreen ordinance.
    • County funding for coastal monitoring and reef restoration.
  4. 04

    Honor iwi kūpuna and cultural places.

    From Wainiha to Coco Palms to Wailua, we keep learning the same lesson the hard way: build on sacred ground, and you break trust. I’ll require real pre-construction cultural review countywide, fund county cultural-resource staff, and stand with Native Hawaiian families defending ancestral sites.

  5. 05

    Food grown here, by people who live here.

    A real food economy is a security issue, a climate issue, and an equity issue. I’ll expand long-term leases for small diversified farmers, push county procurement toward local food in schools and hospitals, and keep fighting for transparency around pesticide use near homes and schools.

  6. 06

    Transit, paths, and a highway that doesn’t break.

    Widening Kūhiō is not a plan. Multimodal is. I’ll fight for an accelerated Ke Ala Hele Makalae, expanded Kauaʻi Bus service for Eastside and Westside working hours, and a Kūhiō corridor redesign that includes protected bike lanes and bus priority.

  7. 07

    Mental health, housing-first, harm reduction.

    Meth and now fentanyl are in our community. Criminalizing our way out hasn’t worked. I support mobile crisis response (not KPD) for mental-health calls, housing-first with wraparound services, naloxone access, and a permanent supportive-housing pipeline.

Record

Two decades of showing up for Kauaʻi — before the title.

2010

Hawaiʻi bans the shark-fin trade — first in the nation.

Partnered with Pew Charitable Trusts to pass the state law that became the model for the rest of the U.S. and dozens of countries.

2011

Federal Shark Conservation Act signed into law.

Lobbied Capitol Hill alongside other shark-attack survivors. Signed by President Obama, closing federal loopholes on finning.

2012–’20

United Nations & global marine sanctuaries.

Testified and lobbied at the U.N. with a coalition of survivors to expand protected waters around the world.

2023

“Shark: Portraits” — Rizzoli, New York.

Eight years of underwater work across Hawaiʻi, Mexico, the Bahamas, Tahiti and beyond — a book that changed how people see an animal.

Why 2026

Four of seven seats are open. This is the election that decides the next decade.

For the first time since 2002, Kauaʻi County Council has four open seats in a single cycle. The mayor’s office changes hands. The laws we pass — on housing, on tourism, on the shoreline — will shape Kauaʻi long past our time on the Council.

I’m running because I’ve spent a lifetime on this island, in these waters, and on the public record. I know how to take a hard fight, build a coalition, and win it — not for an ideology, but for a place.
